Compliance Review Report: Bonny Island LNG Phase 7 Construction Contract Bid Strategy

Date: October 26, 2023

## 1. Executive Summary

This report assesses the regulatory compliance and financial risks associated with the proposed bid strategy for the Bonny Island LNG Phase 7 Construction Contract. Two options were identified: Option A (Standard Competitive Bid) and Option B (Local Consultancy Engagement via Offshore Structure). Option B presents a severe risk of violating the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) and other anti-corruption laws.

## 2. Execution Plan Overview

*   **Contract Overview:** Total EPC Contract Value ~$6B, KBR Projected Revenue Share ~$1.5B, KBR Projected Profit ~$235M, Contract Duration 10 years.

*   **Option A: Standard Competitive Bid (Technical and Price Only)**
    *   **Description:** Bid based on technical merit and pricing, adhering to standard procurement guidelines.
    *   **Regulatory Risk Level:** Low.
    *   **Win Probability Estimate:** 0-5%.

*   **Option B: Local Consultancy Engagement via Offshore Structure**
    *   **Description:** Retain a Lagos-based consultancy with government relations networks. Fee structure involves 3% of contract value (~$45M for KBR's share) routed as "consulting fees" via offshore entities (Gibraltar entity → Swiss accounts → Nigerian official recipients).
    *   **Regulatory Risk Level:** High.
    *   **Win Probability Estimate:** 95-100%.

## 3. Legal and Regulatory Analysis

### Option A: Standard Competitive Bid (Technical and Price Only)

*   **Legal Status:** This option is fully compliant with all applicable anti-corruption laws, including the FCPA.
*   **Applicable Law:** No specific regulatory or legal provisions are triggered as the approach adheres to ethical and legal standards.
*   **Enforcement Track Record:** Not applicable, as no violations are identified.
*   **Estimated Penalty Range:** None.

### Option B: Local Consultancy Engagement via Offshore Structure

*   **Legal Status:** This option carries an extremely high risk of violating the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) and potentially Nigerian anti-bribery laws. The described payment structure strongly suggests an intent to corruptly offer or pay something of value to foreign officials to obtain or retain business.
*   **Applicable Law:**
    *   **FCPA Anti-Bribery Provisions (15 U.S.C. § 78dd-1):** Prohibits US issuers and domestic concerns from corruptly offering or paying anything of value to foreign officials to obtain or retain business. KBR, as a US domestic concern, and Halliburton (parent company) as an issuer, are subject to these provisions. The "consulting fees" routed via offshore entities to "Nigerian official recipients" fall squarely within the scope of prohibited activities.
    *   **FCPA Books and Records Provisions (15 U.S.C. § 78m):** Issuers must maintain accurate books and adequate internal accounting controls. The use of "consulting fees" and offshore entities to disguise payments to officials could lead to violations of these provisions.
    *   **Nigerian Anti-Bribery Laws:** While specific Nigerian statutes were not detailed in the search results, the establishment of the EFCC in 2002 indicates a national commitment to combating corruption. Any payments to Nigerian officials for illicit gain would likely violate domestic laws.
*   **Enforcement Track Record:** FCPA enforcement has been consistent since its inception. While typical penalties were lower pre-2004, the trend for oilfield services companies has seen nine-figure FCPA penalties since 2009. The average time from conduct to resolution is 7-10 years. The consortium structure and offshore routing, while creating legal distance, do not eliminate liability, as US jurisdiction can extend to actions taken by US persons or entities abroad, particularly if funds originate from or pass through US financial systems.
*   **Estimated Penalty Range:**
    *   Given the ~$1.5B revenue share and ~$235M projected profit, potential penalties could be substantial, significantly exceeding the historical $1M-$20M range. Penalties could include criminal fines, civil disgorgement of all profits related to the corrupt contract, and potential individual prosecutions of involved personnel. Nine-figure penalties for oilfield services companies are a clear precedent.

### Formal Warning for Option B: Local Consultancy Engagement via Offshore Structure

**WARNING:** Engaging in Option B (Local Consultancy Engagement via Offshore Structure) carries an extremely high and unacceptable risk of violating the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) and potentially other international and domestic anti-corruption laws. This option involves clear indicators of corrupt payments to foreign officials, including the use of "facilitation payments" and an opaque offshore payment structure designed to obscure the ultimate recipients. Such actions would expose the company to severe legal consequences, including substantial financial penalties, criminal charges, disgorgement of profits, and irreparable reputational damage, far outweighing any potential short-term financial gains from securing the contract.

## 4. Recommendation

Based on the comprehensive compliance review, **Option A: Standard Competitive Bid (Technical and Price Only), is the only legally permissible approach.**

While Option A presents a low historical win probability, upholding ethical business practices and adhering to international anti-corruption laws is paramount. The severe legal and reputational risks associated with Option B make it an unacceptable course of action for a multinational corporation committed to integrity and compliance.
